# Shtriga

> The shtriga is a vampiric witch in Albanian folklore who feeds on the blood of sleeping infants. After feeding she can transform into a flying insect, and the only sure cure for her victims is a remedy obtained from the shtriga herself. Apotropaic protections include garlic, salt, the sign of the cross, and a cross of pig bone placed at a church door on Easter Sunday.

## Overview

The shtriga (Albanian shtrigë) is a figure of Albanian folklore, particularly of the northern Albanian highlands and Kosovo, who appears externally as an old woman but at night feeds on the blood of sleeping infants. The name descends from Latin strix (the owl-witch of Roman tradition), and the shtriga is cognate with the Italian strega, Romanian strigă, Polish strzyga, and South Slavic štriga. After feeding, the shtriga is said to transform into a flying insect — typically a moth, fly, or bee. Children whose blood she has drunk waste away unless cured; only the shtriga who attacked the child can effect a complete cure, often by being persuaded or compelled to spit into the child's mouth. The folklore emphasises strong apotropaic measures: garlic worn or hung at thresholds, the sign of the cross, holy water, salt, and — in the most famous procedure recorded by Edith Durham (1909) — a cross made from the bone of a pig placed at the church door on Easter Sunday so that any shtriga inside could not exit. In Catholic northern Albania the shtriga was held in particularly clear distinction from ordinary witches, with Edith Durham, J. G. von Hahn, and later folklorists such as Robert Elsie providing the principal ethnographic record.

## Theories & Explanations

### Paranormal / Cryptozoological

**Real malign witch** _(Tradition-bearers in northern Albania and Kosovo (recorded by Durham, von Hahn, Elsie))_

Within Albanian highland folklore, the shtriga is a real being — a woman with witch-power obtained through envy or childlessness — capable of supernatural harm.

### Skeptical

**Folkloric explanation of infant illness and death** _(Robert Elsie, Folklore studies of European witch beliefs)_

The shtriga belief offers a culturally meaningful explanation for sudden infant illness, wasting, and death (including conditions later understood as SIDS, infectious disease, and malnutrition) and a mechanism (apotropaic ritual) for community response.

**Strix-derived cultural complex** _(Comparative European folklorists)_

The shtriga is one of a family of European cognate figures (strega, strigoi, strzyga, štriga) descended from Roman strix folklore, transmitted across the Balkans and Mediterranean.
